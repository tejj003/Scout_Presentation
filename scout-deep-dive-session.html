How to use Scout

A practical way to use Scout every day.

Use this pattern whenever the work is more than a simple answer: ask clearly, add context, let Scout act, review the output, and iterate.

Mental model

The Scout loop: ask, ground, build, verify.

Ask

Describe the job, the audience, the format, and the decision you need.

Ground

Add the notes, files, messages, links, or screenshots Scout should use.

Build

Let Scout create the draft, document, summary, workflow, or checked output.

Verify

Review the result, ask for evidence, and approve only what you trust.

Special feature · Coding and files

For code and files, use Scout like a careful teammate.

Ask it to understand the local context, preserve unrelated work, make focused edits, run the right validation, and summarize the result.

Find the owner

Trace the behavior to the file, function, test, or command that controls it.

Change narrowly

Use the repo's style, avoid unrelated cleanup, and keep the edit easy to review.

Run evidence

Prefer a focused test, build, typecheck, lint, or browser check over guesswork.

Explain the delta

Report what changed, what passed, and what risk remains.

Live demo flow

A simple flow you can reuse after this session.

Start with a real task, give Scout the right context, let it build or act, then verify the outcome before you share or approve anything.

Guardrails

Stay in control while Scout does more.

Grant only needed access

Give file, shell, browser, and M365 access intentionally for the job at hand.

Preview what leaves

Review email, Teams, calendar, and other outbound content before it is sent.

Protect sensitive context

Keep private schedules, mail, files, and confidential details out of public output.

Ask for explanations

Have Scout say what it did, what it used, and where uncertainty remains.

Require evidence

Use previews, tests, screenshots, diffs, source links, or tool results as proof.

Keep judgment human

Humans approve risky, external, sensitive, or irreversible steps.
